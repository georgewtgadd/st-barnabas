Metastatic Spinal Cord Compression (MSCC)

MSCC is an oncological emergency. Early identification and prompt action can mean the difference between walking and permanent paralysis. Delay in diagnosis is the most common reason for poor outcomes.

🚨
If you suspect MSCC — act within hours, not days

NICE guidance (NG127) requires all suspected MSCC to be treated as an emergency. The patient must be nursed flat with log-rolling precautions until MSCC is excluded by imaging. Do not wait for imaging results before implementing spinal precautions.

🚩 Red Flag Symptoms
  • New or worsening back or neck pain — especially band-like pain radiating around the chest or abdomen
  • "Heavy" or weak legs — difficulty walking, stumbling, or inability to stand
  • Sensory changes — numbness, tingling, or a level below which sensation is reduced
  • Bladder or bowel dysfunction — retention, incontinence, or sudden-onset constipation
📋 Immediate Management Steps
  • Nurse flat — spinal precautions until imaging confirms or excludes compression
  • High-dose dexamethasone — reduces cord oedema (typically 16mg immediately)
  • Urgent MRI whole spine — within 24 hours of suspected diagnosis
  • Refer urgently — to oncology or spinal surgery team immediately
  • Adequate analgesia — ensure pain is controlled throughout
📘
NICE NG127 (2023)

All patients with cancer and new back pain should be assessed for MSCC. Approximately 5% of cancer patients will develop MSCC. The thoracic spine is most commonly affected (70%), followed by lumbar (20%) and cervical (10%) regions.
