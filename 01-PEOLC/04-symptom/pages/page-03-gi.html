Managing the Gastrointestinal Tract

Nausea and dehydration at end of life require a patient-centred approach that challenges common assumptions about nutrition and hydration.

🤢
Managing Nausea

Nausea is multifactorial — causes include constipation, opioids, raised intracranial pressure, or metabolic disturbance. Matching the antiemetic to the cause is more effective than a blanket approach.

  • Review all medications — is an opioid dose adjustment needed?
  • Constipation is a common, treatable cause — assess at every visit
  • Small, frequent, cold meals may be better tolerated than hot food
  • Ginger, acupressure, and upright positioning can all help
💧
Hydration & the Dry Mouth Myth

Evidence consistently shows IV or subcutaneous fluids rarely relieve thirst at end of life and can increase suffering. Thirst is driven by dry mouth — treat the mouth, not the blood tests.

  • IV fluids at end of life risk: pulmonary oedema and prolonged dying
  • Moist swabs, lip balm, and flavoured mouth sprays relieve thirst effectively
  • Families equate fluids with care — a sensitive conversation is essential
  • Mouth care every 1–2 hours provides more relief than a bag of IV fluids
💊
Mouth Care Over IV Fluids

A simple protocol — moist swabs every 1–2 hours, petroleum jelly for lips, flavoured mouth sprays — provides more relief than IV hydration at end of life. More importantly, it keeps families involved in active, meaningful care at the bedside.

⚠️
When Clinically Assisted Hydration May Be Appropriate

There are situations where hydration is clinically justified — when opioid toxicity is suspected, when the patient remains alert and expresses thirst, or where reversible dehydration from a treatable cause is the primary problem. The decision must always be individualised and re-evaluated regularly.
