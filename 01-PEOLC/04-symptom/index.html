Managing the Respiratory Tract

Breathlessness (dyspnoea) and terminal secretions are two of the most distressing respiratory symptoms in palliative care — often more frightening for the family than for the patient themselves.

💧
Terminal Secretions — the "Death Rattle"

As patients become unable to swallow, secretions pool in the throat producing a gurgling sound. Research consistently shows this is rarely distressing to the unconscious patient — but it can be deeply upsetting for families. Your role includes explaining this clearly and sensitively.

Managing the Gastrointestinal Tract

Nausea and dehydration at end of life require a patient-centred approach that challenges some common assumptions about nutrition and hydration.

🤢
Managing Nausea

Nausea is multifactorial in palliative care — causes include constipation, opioids, raised intracranial pressure, or metabolic disturbance. Matching the antiemetic to the cause (e.g. haloperidol for opioid-induced nausea) is more effective than a one-size-fits-all approach.

  • Review all medications — is an opioid dose adjustment needed?
  • Constipation is a common, treatable cause — assess bowel function at every visit
  • Small, frequent, cold meals may be better tolerated than hot food
  • Ginger, acupressure, and positioning (upright after eating) can all help
💧
Hydration & the Dry Mouth Myth

One of the most important clinical conversations in end-of-life care is about artificial hydration. Evidence consistently shows that IV or subcutaneous fluids rarely relieve thirst at end of life and can increase suffering.

  • Thirst is driven by dry mouth, not dehydration — treat the mouth, not the blood tests
  • IV fluids at end of life risk: pulmonary oedema, peripheral oedema, and prolonged dying
  • Regular mouth care (moistened swabs, lip balm) relieves thirst and dry mouth effectively
  • Families often equate fluids with care — a sensitive conversation about quality over quantity is essential
🩺
Clinical Pearl: Mouth Care Over IV Fluids

A simple protocol — moist swabs every 1–2 hours, petroleum jelly for lips, flavoured mouth sprays — has been shown to provide more relief than a bag of IV fluids. More importantly, it keeps the family involved in active, meaningful care at the bedside.

⚠️
When Clinically Assisted Hydration May Be Appropriate

There are situations where hydration is clinically justified — for example, when opioid toxicity is suspected, when the patient remains alert and expresses thirst, or where reversible dehydration (e.g. from a treatable cause such as vomiting) is the primary problem. The decision must always be individualised and re-evaluated regularly.

Palliative Emergencies

A palliative emergency is a sudden change in a patient's condition that, if not recognised and managed promptly, will cause extreme distress or rapid irreversible deterioration.

Not every change in a palliative patient's condition is an emergency. Knowing the difference between expected deterioration and a clinical emergency requiring urgent action is a fundamental skill — and can mean the difference between comfort and catastrophe.

Terminal Haemorrhage

Catastrophic haemorrhage is rare but can occur when a tumour erodes a major vessel — most commonly in head and neck cancers or those involving the carotid artery. It requires a calm, structured response.

🩸
At-Risk Patients

Patients with head and neck tumours, or those with a history of haemoptysis (coughing blood) or melaena, are at higher risk. Anticipatory medication (typically Midazolam IM/SC) should already be prescribed. Dark-coloured towels should be kept available in the home environment.

Hypercalcaemia

Malignant hypercalcaemia occurs most commonly in squamous cell lung cancer and breast cancer. It is the most common metabolic emergency in oncology and is both treatable and life-limiting.

Hypercalcaemia can be remembered using the mnemonic "Stones, Bones, Groans, and Moans" — a systematic reminder of the four domains of symptoms. Click each section below to reveal the clinical features.

🪨
Stones
Renal / Urinary
  • Polyuria (excessive urination)
  • Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
  • Nephrocalcinosis (calcium deposits in kidney)
  • Renal stones (in chronic cases)
High calcium reduces kidney's ability to concentrate urine, leading to dehydration and a vicious cycle of worsening hypercalcaemia.

💊
Treatment: IV Bisphosphonates

Confirmed malignant hypercalcaemia (corrected serum calcium >2.6 mmol/L) is treated with IV hydration followed by IV bisphosphonate (typically Zoledronic acid or Pamidronate). The calcium-lowering effect takes 2–4 days. It is a reversible emergency — but in advanced cancer, it often recurs and may signal a shift in prognosis.

Metastatic Spinal Cord Compression (MSCC)

MSCC is an oncological emergency. Early identification and prompt action can mean the difference between walking and permanent paralysis. Delay in diagnosis is the most common reason for poor outcomes.

🚨
If you suspect MSCC — act within hours, not days

NICE guidance (NG127) recommends that all suspected MSCC is treated as an emergency. The patient should be nursed flat with log-rolling precautions until MSCC is excluded. Do not wait for imaging before implementing spinal precautions.

🚩 Red Flag Symptoms
  • New or worsening back or neck pain — especially band-like pain that radiates around the chest or abdomen
  • "Heavy" or weak legs — difficulty walking, stumbling, or inability to stand
  • Sensory changes — numbness, tingling, or a "level" below which sensation is reduced
  • Bladder or bowel dysfunction — retention, incontinence, or constipation of sudden onset
📋
Immediate Management
  • Nurse flat — spinal precautions until imaging confirms or excludes compression
  • High-dose dexamethasone — reduces spinal cord oedema (typically 16mg immediately)
  • Urgent MRI whole spine — within 24 hours of suspected diagnosis
  • Refer urgently — to oncology or spinal surgery team immediately
  • Pain control — ensure adequate analgesia throughout
📘 NICE NG127 (2023)

All patients with cancer and new back pain should be assessed for MSCC. Approximately 5% of cancer patients will develop MSCC. The thoracic spine is most commonly affected (70%), followed by lumbar (20%) and cervical (10%) regions.

Superior Vena Cava Obstruction (SVCO)

SVCO occurs when a tumour in the mediastinum compresses the superior vena cava, obstructing venous return from the upper body. It presents dramatically and requires urgent treatment.

SVCO most commonly occurs in lung cancer and mediastinal lymphoma. Symptoms develop because blood cannot drain from the head, neck, and arms back to the heart. Click on the numbered areas of the diagram below to learn about each clinical feature.

① Facial & Periorbital Oedema

Progressive swelling of the face, particularly around the eyes, is one of the most visible and distressing signs of SVCO. Patients often describe their face feeling "full" or "puffy." The conjunctivae may appear reddened.

Often worse in the morning — fluid redistributes when the patient has been lying flat overnight.
② Distended Neck Veins

Non-pulsatile distension of the external jugular veins, visible even when the patient is sitting upright. This occurs because venous blood cannot drain past the obstruction. In severe cases, veins may be visible across the chest wall.

Distinguish from pulsatile JVP elevation (which suggests cardiac causes) — in SVCO the veins are non-pulsatile.
③ Arm Swelling (Bilateral)

Swelling of both arms occurs as venous drainage from the upper limbs is obstructed. The skin may feel tense and pitting oedema is common. Patients may find it difficult to wear clothing or jewellery.

IV access in the arms should be avoided — venous pressure is elevated and infusions may not drain effectively.
④ Breathlessness & Chest Pain

Compression of airways by the mediastinal tumour causes dyspnoea, which may be severe. Patients often report a sense of suffocation. Chest pain may be present if the pleura or pericardium is involved.

Treatment: High-dose dexamethasone, urgent radiotherapy or stenting, and position the patient upright to reduce venous engorgement.

Managing Seizures

Seizures in palliative patients may occur due to brain metastases, metabolic disturbance, or medication. Management in the community or hospice requires a calm, structured approach.

⏱️
When to Intervene

A generalised tonic-clonic seizure lasting less than 5 minutes should be observed and the patient kept safe but does not require emergency medication. A seizure lasting 5 minutes or more (or two seizures without recovery between them) constitutes status epilepticus and requires immediate pharmacological intervention.
