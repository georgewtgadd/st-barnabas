Legal Protections

Understanding the hierarchy of decision-making when a patient loses capacity — and a common misconception that puts patients at risk.

⚠️ Common Misconception: In UK law, the label "Next of Kin" carries no legal standing to make healthcare decisions for an incapacitated adult. It is a social term only. Despite this, it is frequently and incorrectly treated as granting decision-making authority. Relying on a Next of Kin without checking for an LPA or ADRT is a clinical and legal error.

Hierarchy of decision-making for incapacitated adults
1
Advance Decision to Refuse Treatment (ADRT)

A legally binding document (if valid and applicable) in which the patient, while having capacity, records their refusal of specific treatments in specific circumstances. Must be in writing and witnessed to refuse life-sustaining treatment.

Legally Binding
2
Health & Welfare Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA)

A legally appointed person (the 'donee') authorised to make health and welfare decisions on behalf of the patient once capacity is lost. Must be registered with the Office of the Public Guardian before it is valid. The donee can consent to or refuse treatment — but cannot override a valid ADRT.

Legally Binding
3
Court-Appointed Deputy

Appointed by the Court of Protection where there is no LPA and the person lacks capacity for major decisions. Rarely encountered in clinical practice but relevant for complex or contested cases. The Deputy's powers are set by the Court order.

Legally Binding
4
Best Interests Decision by Clinician / MDT

Where no valid ADRT, LPA or Court Deputy exists, the treating clinician — supported by the MDT — makes a Best Interests decision under Section 4 of the MCA (2005). This must involve consultation with those close to the patient and consideration of past wishes.

Clinical Process
5
Next of Kin / Family Members

Family members and carers are important consultees in a Best Interests decision — their knowledge of the patient's past wishes, beliefs and values is invaluable. However, they have no legal authority to make or refuse treatment decisions unless they hold a registered Health & Welfare LPA.

No Legal Standing

Practice point: Always check for a valid ADRT or registered LPA on admission and when capacity changes. Document your findings clearly. If an ADRT is present, read it carefully — it is only valid and applicable if the specific treatment being considered matches what the document describes.
