Principle 1
Presumption of Capacity

Principle 1
Clinical Example

A nurse assumes a patient with dementia cannot decide their preferred place of care. This violates Principle 1. Capacity must be assessed for this specific decision before concluding it is absent.

Principle 2
Support to Make Decisions
Click to see a clinical example
Principle 2
Clinical Example

Before assessing a patient with aphasia, the team provides an easy-read leaflet and an interpreter. All practicable steps to support decision-making must be taken before concluding capacity is lacking.

Principle 3
Unwise Decisions ≠ Lack of Capacity
Click to see a clinical example
Principle 3
Clinical Example

A patient with capacity refuses life-saving dialysis. Even if the clinical team disagrees, this decision must be respected — provided the patient demonstrates they understand the consequences of refusal.

Principle 4
Act in Best Interests
Click to see a clinical example
Principle 4
Clinical Example

For a patient who has lost capacity, the MDT consults the family and reviews past documented wishes before deciding to withdraw artificial nutrition. The decision must reflect what the patient would have wanted — not what others prefer.

Principle 5
Least Restrictive Option
Click to see a clinical example
Principle 5
Clinical Example

When deciding between a PEG tube and careful hand feeding for a patient with dementia, the team must consider which option least restricts the patient's rights and freedom — invasive interventions require stronger justification under this principle.

Key point: The MCA applies to any decision — from day-to-day choices about personal care through to major clinical decisions about treatment. Capacity is always decision-specific and time-specific — a person may have capacity for one decision but not another, and their capacity may fluctuate over time.
