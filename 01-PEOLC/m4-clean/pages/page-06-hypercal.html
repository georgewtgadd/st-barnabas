Hypercalcaemia

Malignant hypercalcaemia is most common in squamous cell lung cancer and breast cancer. It is the most common metabolic emergency in oncology — and it is treatable.

Remembered using the mnemonic "Stones, Bones, Groans & Moans" — a systematic reminder of the four clinical domains. Click each card to reveal the features and underlying mechanism.
🪨
Stones
Renal / Urinary
↓
  • Polyuria (excessive urination)
  • Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
  • Nephrocalcinosis (calcium deposits in the kidney)
High calcium reduces the kidney's ability to concentrate urine, creating a vicious cycle of dehydration and worsening hypercalcaemia.
🦴
Bones
Musculoskeletal
↓
  • Deep, aching bone pain
  • Pathological fractures
  • Proximal muscle weakness
Osteolytic metastases release calcium directly from bone; PTHrP secreted by tumours stimulates osteoclastic bone resorption.
😫
Groans
Gastrointestinal
↓
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Constipation (the most common GI feature)
  • Abdominal pain and anorexia
Calcium reduces smooth muscle excitability, slowing GI motility — contributing to constipation, nausea, and bloating.
😵
Moans
Neuropsychiatric
↓
  • Confusion and cognitive impairment
  • Lethargy and profound fatigue
  • Depression, anxiety, and in severe cases — coma
Calcium affects neuronal membrane potentials and neurotransmitter release, causing CNS depression from mild confusion to coma.
💊
Treatment: IV Bisphosphonates

Confirmed malignant hypercalcaemia (corrected serum calcium >2.6 mmol/L) is treated with IV hydration followed by IV bisphosphonate (Zoledronic acid or Pamidronate). The calcium-lowering effect takes 2–4 days. In advanced cancer it often recurs and may signal a shift in prognosis.
