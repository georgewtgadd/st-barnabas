In palliative and end of life care, how we convey a message is often more significant than the words themselves.

Albert Mehrabian — Communication Research
The 7-38-55 Rule

When communicating feelings or attitudes, our overall impact is divided across three channels. Understanding this helps us to be intentional about all aspects of how we communicate — not just our words.

7%
Words
What we actually say
38%
Tone
Pitch, speed, and volume
55%
Body Language
Posture, eye contact, facial expressions
Barriers to effective conversation
🩹
Physical Barriers

The patient is in pain, fatigued, or has sensory loss such as impaired hearing or vision. These make it difficult to focus on or retain complex information.

🏥
Environmental Barriers

Lack of privacy, noisy wards, physical barriers like high hospital beds, or poor positioning all undermine the non-verbal signals we rely on for sensitive communication.

🐘
Psychological Barriers

The "Elephant in the Room" — fear of causing distress, saying the wrong thing, or a professional's own discomfort with death and dying can prevent honest conversations.

Why this matters in palliative care: Patients and families are highly attuned to non-verbal signals when facing a life-limiting diagnosis. A clinician who stands at the door (body language) and speaks quickly (tone) communicates urgency and discomfort — regardless of how carefully their words are chosen. Being deliberate about all three channels is an act of professional compassion.
