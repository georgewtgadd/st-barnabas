Legal Protections & ACP Tools

Understanding the hierarchy of decision-making when a patient loses capacity, and the documents used to record their wishes in advance.

⚠️ Common Misconception: In UK law, the label "Next of Kin" carries no legal standing to make healthcare decisions for an incapacitated adult. It is a social term only. Relying on a Next of Kin without checking for an LPA or ADRT is a clinical and legal error.

Hierarchy of decision-making for incapacitated adults
1
Advance Decision to Refuse Treatment (ADRT)

A legally binding document (if valid and applicable) in which the patient, while having capacity, records their refusal of specific treatments in specific circumstances. Must be in writing and witnessed to refuse life-sustaining treatment.

Legally Binding
2
Health & Welfare Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA)

A legally appointed person authorised to make health and welfare decisions on behalf of the patient once capacity is lost. Must be registered with the Office of the Public Guardian before it is valid. Cannot override a valid ADRT.

Legally Binding
3
Best Interests Decision by Clinician / MDT

Where no valid ADRT or LPA exists, the treating clinician — supported by the MDT — makes a Best Interests decision under Section 4 of the MCA (2005), consulting those close to the patient.

Clinical Process
4
Next of Kin / Family Members

Important consultees in a Best Interests decision, but with no legal authority to make or refuse treatment decisions unless they hold a registered Health & Welfare LPA.

No Legal Standing
Advance Care Planning — key documents at a glance
📄 ADRT
Legally Binding

Allows a person to legally refuse specific treatments in future circumstances. Written, signed and witnessed — binds the clinical team when valid and applicable.

📋 ReSPECT Form
Clinical Recommendation

Summarises recommended care priorities for emergency situations. Guides emergency clinicians when time is short. Not legally binding, but clinically significant.

🏡 Preferred Place of Care
Patient Wish

Records where a person would prefer to be cared for and to die. Not legally binding, but should meaningfully inform all care planning decisions.

Practice point: Always check for a valid ADRT or registered LPA on admission and when capacity changes. The ReSPECT process should be completed with the patient, not done to them — it is a conversation, not just a form.
