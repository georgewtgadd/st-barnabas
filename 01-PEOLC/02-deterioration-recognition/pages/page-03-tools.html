Supportive & Palliative Care Indicators Tool
SPICT

A validated trigger tool developed at the University of Edinburgh. Identifies people whose health is deteriorating who may benefit from holistic needs assessment and advance care planning.

All settings2022
When & Why We Use SPICT
When you would not be surprised if the patient died in the next 12 months (the Surprise Question)
At any unplanned admission or GP contact for a life-limiting condition
Triggers a holistic review — supports clinical judgement, does not replace it
Result prompts: review medications, agree care plan, consider specialist referral

Australia-modified Karnofsky Performance Scale
AKPS

A validated 11-point functional status scale used to rate a patient's ability to perform everyday tasks and track functional trajectory over time.

AKPS ≤50 = significant deterioration
When & Why We Use the AKPS
At every clinical encounter — document and compare with previous scores
A declining score over time is a more reliable indicator than a single snapshot
AKPS ≤30 is associated with prognosis of weeks, not months
Use alongside other tools — AKPS measures function, not symptom burden alone

GSF Proactive Identification Guidance (7th Edition)
PIG Tool

Enables earlier identification of people who may need additional supportive care as they near end of life. Applicable to all conditions, all settings, and all care providers.

Three-step framework2 pages
When & Why We Use the PIG Tool
Step 1: Surprise Question — would you be surprised if this patient died in the next year?
Designed for proactive use — before a crisis, not in response to one
Earlier identification leads to better planning and fewer crisis admissions
Positive result adds patient to a palliative care register, triggering proactive care

Rockwood Clinical Frailty Scale
Clinical Frailty Scale

A validated 9-point frailty assessment. Scores ≥5 indicate significant frailty; scores ≥7 may indicate the patient is approaching end of life and should prompt ACP review.

CFS ≥7 = approaching EOL
When & Why We Use the Clinical Frailty Scale
Particularly useful in older adults and those with dementia or multiple long-term conditions
CFS ≥7 warrants a care plan review including advance care planning
Rate of change across repeated assessments is as important as the absolute score
Guides conversations about goals of care, treatment escalation, and preferred place of death

World Health Organization
Analgesic Ladder

The three-step WHO analgesic ladder provides a structured framework for cancer pain management — guiding appropriate analgesia from non-opioid through to strong opioid therapy with adjuvants at each step.

Pain management3-step framework
When & Why We Use the Analgesic Ladder
Step 1: Non-opioids (paracetamol, NSAIDs) ± adjuvants for mild pain
Step 2: Mild opioids (codeine, tramadol) ± non-opioids for moderate pain
Step 3: Strong opioids (morphine, oxycodone) for severe pain — titrate to effect
Adjuvants (steroids, antidepressants, anticonvulsants) can be added at any step

Palliative Care Assessment
Pain Assessment Model

A structured model for assessing pain in palliative care settings. Recognises that pain is multidimensional — accounting for physical, psychological, social, and spiritual components of a person's total pain experience.

Total pain model4 dimensions
When & Why We Use the Pain Assessment Model
At every visit where pain is reported or suspected — do not rely on self-report alone
Physical: site, radiation, severity, character, timing, aggravating and relieving factors
Psychological: anxiety, depression, fear of dying — all amplify the pain experience
Social & spiritual dimensions must be assessed — unresolved distress worsens total pain
