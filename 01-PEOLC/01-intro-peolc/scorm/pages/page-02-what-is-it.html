What is Palliative Care?

Understanding the full scope of palliative care — not just what it is, but what it is not.

Palliative care is an approach that improves the quality of life of patients and their families facing the problems associated with life-threatening illness, through the prevention and relief of suffering by means of early identification and treatment of pain and other physical, psychosocial and spiritual problems.
— World Health Organization (WHO)
🕐
Not just the final days

Palliative care can begin at or around the time of diagnosis of a life-limiting condition — not only in the last days or weeks of life.

🏥
Not only in hospices

Palliative care is delivered in hospitals, care homes, and people's own homes, as well as in hospices.

💊
Not giving up treatment

Palliative care is given alongside active treatments such as chemotherapy or radiotherapy — it complements, not replaces, curative intent.

👨‍👩‍👧
Includes families & carers

Palliative care explicitly extends support to families and carers, including into bereavement where needed.

🌿
Holistic & person-centred

It addresses physical, psychological, social and spiritual needs — the whole person, not just their condition.

👥
A whole-team approach

Good palliative care is delivered by a multidisciplinary team — coordinated across primary care, secondary care and community services.
