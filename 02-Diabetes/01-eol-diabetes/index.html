Clinical & Ethical Dilemmas
The Core Tension

Managing diabetes at end of life requires a fundamental shift in philosophy — away from long-term prevention of complications (retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy) and toward immediate comfort and dignity. HbA1c targets become irrelevant; symptom experience becomes paramount.

Avoiding Acute Harm

Hypoglycaemia causes distressing symptoms — tremor, confusion, sweating, loss of consciousness — that are wholly incompatible with a peaceful death. DKA causes nausea, vomiting and air hunger. Both are preventable with appropriate dose reduction and targeted monitoring.

The Burden of Monitoring

Frequent finger-prick testing is painful and medicalising. As a patient declines, the benefit of tight glucose control falls to zero while the burden remains. In the final days, monitoring frequency should be reduced or discontinued unless symptoms suggest acute hypo or hyper episodes requiring intervention.

Treat vs. Withdraw

This is rarely a binary decision. In practice, clinicians modulate therapy: reducing insulin doses, stopping oral hypoglycaemics, ceasing statins and antihypertensives, while retaining subcutaneous insulin at low doses to prevent DKA in insulin-dependent patients. The goal is the minimum intervention required for comfort.
