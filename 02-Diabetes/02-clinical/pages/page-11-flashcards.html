Patient Presentation
Patient is cool and clammy to touch, visibly anxious, shaking, and has become suddenly confused over the last 10 minutes.
Tap to reveal →
✓ Diagnosis
Hypoglycaemia
Immediate Action

If safe to swallow: give 150–200ml of a sugary drink (fruit juice, Lucozade) or glucose tablets immediately. Retest in 10–15 minutes.

If unable to swallow: consider Glucagon IM/SC or call 999. Do not attempt to give anything by mouth to an unconscious patient.

2
Patient Presentation
Patient has deep, laboured breathing and their breath smells of "pear drops". They are extremely thirsty and have been very drowsy since this morning.
Tap to reveal →
✓ Diagnosis
Hyperglycaemia / DKA
Immediate Action

Check the Advance Care Plan immediately. DKA cannot be managed in a community setting.

If the patient wishes comfort care at home: focus on symptom control, call the palliative team.

If hospital transfer is in keeping with their wishes: call 999 and document clinical findings.

3
Patient Presentation
During a routine visit, the patient seems more drowsy than usual and their carer reports they haven't eaten since yesterday. They are on a Sulphonylurea.
Tap to reveal →
✓ Likely Diagnosis
Hypoglycaemia (risk)
Immediate Action

Test blood glucose immediately. A patient on a sulphonylurea who hasn't eaten is at very high hypoglycaemia risk — the drug continues stimulating insulin regardless of intake.

If blood glucose is below 4.0 mmol/L, treat as hypoglycaemia. Contact the prescriber urgently to review/stop the sulphonylurea.

4
Patient Presentation
An elderly Type 2 patient has become increasingly confused over three days. Very high blood glucose reading (34 mmol/L). No fruity breath. Severely dehydrated.
Tap to reveal →
✓ Diagnosis
HHS — Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State
Immediate Action

HHS develops slowly and is an emergency requiring hospital-level IV fluid management.

Check the Advance Care Plan before transferring. The absence of ketones and fruity breath distinguishes this from DKA. Gradual, careful rehydration is key — rapid correction can cause cerebral oedema.
