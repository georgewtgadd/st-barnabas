Emergency Management

Managing Hyperglycaemia & Emergencies

Severe hyperglycaemia can escalate to two life-threatening emergencies. Understanding the signs and the limits of community management is critical — but so is respecting the patient's documented wishes.

DKA
Diabetic Ketoacidosis
Primarily Type 1 — also SGLT2 inhibitor-associated
  • Extreme thirst and dry mouth
  • Polyuria (frequent, large-volume urination)
  • Deep, laboured breathing (Kussmaul respirations)
  • Fruity/pear-drop smelling breath (ketones)
  • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
  • Confusion or altered consciousness

⚠️ Note: SGLT2 inhibitors can cause euglycaemic DKA — ketoacidosis with near-normal blood glucose readings.

HHS
Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State
Primarily Type 2 — typically in older patients
  • Very high blood glucose (often 30+ mmol/L)
  • Severe dehydration
  • Progressive confusion and drowsiness
  • Seizures (in severe cases)
  • Weakness and lethargy
  • No ketones / no fruity breath (distinguishes from DKA)

    • ⚠️ HHS develops slowly over days to weeks. It is often the first presentation of undiagnosed Type 2 Diabetes in elderly patients.

    📋
    Critical Decision Point
    Management Depends on the Advance Care Plan

    DKA and HHS cannot be managed in a community setting or standard hospice unit — they require acute hospital care with IV fluids, electrolyte management, and continuous monitoring. Whether to pursue that care depends entirely on what the patient has documented.

    ✓ Comfort Focus at Home If the patient has documented a wish to remain at home with comfort-focused care, hospital transfer is not appropriate. Focus on symptom management — hydration via subcutaneous fluids if indicated, and analgesia.
    → Hospital Transfer If the patient has not excluded hospital admission and the clinical picture warrants it, transfer for acute management. Discuss clearly with family that this is emergency treatment, not a change in the overall care plan.
