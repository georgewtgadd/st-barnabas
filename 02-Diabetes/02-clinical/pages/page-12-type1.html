Special Consideration

Type 1 Diabetes at End of Life

Type 1 Diabetes requires a fundamentally different approach at end of life compared to Type 2. Insulin is not a drug of choice — it is a physiological necessity. Withdrawal is not rationalisation; it can be fatal.

⚠️
Critical Warning
Do Not Withhold Insulin Without Specialist Advice

In Type 1 Diabetes, the body produces no insulin whatsoever. Without exogenous insulin, glucose cannot enter cells, fatty acids are mobilised, and ketones accumulate — causing DKA within hours to days. This is a painful, distressing, and preventable death if insulin is withdrawn carelessly.

Principle 1
Continue Insulin — Adjust, Don't Stop

Insulin should generally be continued as long as the patient remains conscious. As appetite and food intake decline, doses must be reduced — but the drug itself should not be withdrawn without specialist guidance.

  • ✓ Reduce dose as oral intake falls to prevent hypoglycaemia
  • ✓ Switch to once-daily long-acting insulin if multiple daily doses become burdensome
  • ✓ Monitor for signs of both hypo and DKA as clinical status changes
  • ✗ Do not stop insulin entirely without specialist diabetologist input
  • ✗ Do not apply the same "stop non-essential medications" logic that applies to statins and antihypertensives
Principle 2
Dose Adjustment Is Active Care

Adjusting insulin doses as a patient deteriorates is not a withdrawal of care — it is proactive, compassionate management. The goal shifts from glycaemic control to preventing the acute distress of both hypoglycaemia and ketoacidosis.

  • ✓ If the patient cannot eat: reduce basal insulin by 20–30% and stop bolus insulin
  • ✓ Document every dose change and the clinical rationale clearly
  • ✓ Involve the diabetology team early — anticipate the need for dose reduction
📞

When in doubt, seek specialist advice. The regional diabetes team or palliative medicine consultant can advise on insulin regimens for dying patients. You do not need to manage this alone.
