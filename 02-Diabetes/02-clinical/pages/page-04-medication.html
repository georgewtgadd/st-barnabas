Medication Safety

Medication Review: "Stop and Think"

Non-insulin drug therapies must be reviewed as the patient deteriorates. Many medications that were essential months ago now carry more risk than benefit.

⬛ Consider Stopping
Metformin

Consider stopping if the patient has gastrointestinal disturbances — nausea, diarrhoea, vomiting — or significantly reduced renal function. In dying patients, reduced fluid intake further compromises renal clearance, raising lactic acidosis risk.

⚠️ High Risk — Review Urgently
Sulphonylureas

Carry a high risk of hypoglycaemia, especially if the patient has a poor appetite or erratic eating pattern. They stimulate insulin secretion regardless of food intake. Should usually be stopped or dose-reduced significantly as oral intake falls.

⚡ Caution — DKA Risk
SGLT2 Inhibitors

Associated with severe, life-threatening diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) — including euglycaemic DKA where blood glucose may appear near-normal. In patients with reduced intake or acute illness, the risk is substantially elevated. Usually stopped at end of life.

⬛ Stop — No Benefit
Statins & Antihypertensives

These medications prevent long-term cardiovascular events over years or decades. At end of life, that timeframe is irrelevant. Remove medications that no longer provide benefit — they add pill burden, side effects, and unnecessary monitoring.

🧭
Guiding Principle

For every medication, ask: "Would stopping this cause harm in the time this patient has left?" If the answer is no — consider stopping it. The goal is to reduce burden, not manage numbers.
