A Fundamental Shift in Goals

As a patient enters the end-of-life phase, the entire purpose of diabetes management changes. Understanding this is the foundation of everything that follows.

⚖️ From long-term prevention → to immediate comfort and dignity
The clinical evidence base for tight glycaemic control is built on preventing complications over years and decades. At end of life, those timescales no longer apply. The goal is no longer a perfect reading — it is a comfortable, dignified death.
📉
Relax Glucose Targets
Tight "normal" ranges are no longer the goal. Long-term complications are irrelevant when prognosis is weeks to months. Aggressive monitoring can cause unnecessary distress and offers no clinical benefit.
🩺
Prioritise Symptom Control
The clinical goal becomes avoiding the distressing extremes — hypoglycaemia (confusion, seizures, loss of consciousness) and severe hyperglycaemia (thirst, polyuria, infections) — rather than achieving tight control.
💊
Rationalise Medications
Many medications that were essential months ago now carry more risk than benefit. A structured medication review should be triggered when a patient enters the terminal phase or when oral intake falls significantly.
📋
Honour Advance Care Plans
All clinical decisions — including whether to treat an emergency — must be guided by the patient's documented wishes. A high blood glucose reading does not override a clearly stated preference to remain at home for comfort care.
⚠️
Trigger Point for Review
A structured medication review should be triggered: when a patient enters the red/terminal phase, when they are no longer eating and drinking, when there is a significant reduction in renal function, or when swallowing medications becomes unsafe.

Blood Glucose Zones

For patients in the terminal phase, glucose targets are deliberately relaxed. The goal is avoiding distressing symptoms at both extremes — not achieving a perfect reading.

20+ mmol/L
⚠️ DKA / HHS Risk
Severe hyperglycaemia — symptoms likely. Risk of DKA (Type 1) or HHS (Type 2). Consider whether hospital-level care aligns with the care plan.
15–20 mmol/L
🟡 Caution Zone
Elevated but not immediately dangerous. Monitor symptoms — thirst, confusion, dry mouth. Review medication regimen. Test once daily or when symptomatic.
6–15 mmol/L
✓ Comfort Target — Terminal Phase
This is the recommended target range. At 6–15 mmol/L, most patients will be free from the symptoms of both extremes. Finger-prick testing should be minimised — once daily or when symptomatic is usually sufficient.
Below 4 mmol/L
🔴 Hypoglycaemia
Treat immediately. Below 4 mmol/L causes confusion, sweating, seizures and loss of consciousness. This is preventable, reversible, and distressing — it must be avoided with appropriate medication rationalisation.
🩸
Testing Frequency
Minimise finger-prick testing. In the final days, once daily — or only when symptomatic — is usually sufficient. Testing causes distress without clinical benefit in the terminal phase.
📋
Symptom-Led Monitoring
If the patient appears suddenly confused, shaky, or disorientated, test first if possible — then treat. In the absence of equipment, treat presumptively based on clinical signs.

Medication Review: Stop & Think

Non-insulin drug therapies must be reviewed as the patient deteriorates. Many medications that were once essential now carry more risk than benefit. This table is your clinical reference.

Drug / Class Recommendation Risk & Rationale
Metformin ⛔ Consider Stopping Reduced fluid intake at end of life impairs renal clearance, raising the risk of lactic acidosis. Stop if nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, or eGFR declining.
Sulphonylureas
(e.g. Gliclazide)		 ⛔ Usually Stop Stimulate insulin secretion regardless of food intake. High risk of hypoglycaemia when a patient is eating poorly or erratically. Should usually be stopped or significantly dose-reduced as oral intake falls.
SGLT2 Inhibitors
(e.g. Empagliflozin)		 ⛔ Stop Can trigger euglycaemic DKA — ketoacidosis with near-normal blood glucose, making it easy to miss. Volume depletion from reduced intake worsens this risk. Stop proactively.
Statins ⛔ Stop Prevent long-term cardiovascular events over years. At end of life, this benefit is irrelevant. Stopping removes pill burden and potential myopathy risk.
Antihypertensives ⚠️ Review Preventing stroke / MI over years is no longer a clinical goal. Continuing may cause symptomatic hypotension, dizziness, and falls. Usually stop unless controlling a specific symptom.
Basal Insulin
(Type 1)		 ✅ Continue — Adjust In Type 1 diabetes, insulin is a physiological necessity, not just a blood glucose tool. Stopping it causes DKA. Doses should be reduced as intake falls — never stopped without specialist advice.
Insulin (Type 2) ⚠️ Review & Reduce May be reduceable or stopped in Type 2 depending on clinical context. If the patient's blood glucose remains within the 6–15 mmol/L comfort range without insulin, stopping may be appropriate — with specialist input.

Hyperglycaemia, Emergencies & Type 1

Severe hyperglycaemia can escalate to two distinct emergencies. Recognising the signs — and knowing when to act — is critical. However, any escalation must always be guided by the patient's Advance Care Plan.

DKA — Diabetic Ketoacidosis

Primarily Type 1 · Also SGLT2-associated

  • Extreme thirst and dry mouth
  • Polyuria (large-volume, frequent urination)
  • Deep, laboured breathing (Kussmaul)
  • Fruity / pear-drop smelling breath
  • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
  • Confusion or altered consciousness
⚠️ SGLT2 inhibitors can cause euglycaemic DKA — ketoacidosis with near-normal glucose readings. Do not rule out DKA based on BG alone.
HHS — Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State

Primarily Type 2 · Often older patients

  • Very high BG — often above 30 mmol/L
  • Progressive drowsiness and confusion
  • Severe dehydration — dry mouth, sunken eyes
  • Develops over days (unlike DKA, which is faster)
  • No significant ketones or acidosis
  • High risk of thrombosis
💡 Both DKA and HHS require hospital-level care. Always check the patient's ACP before escalating.
⚠️ Type 1 Diabetes — A Fundamentally Different Approach
In Type 1, insulin is not a drug of choice — it is a physiological necessity. The body produces no insulin whatsoever. Stopping insulin in a Type 1 patient causes DKA within hours to days. This is a painful, distressing, and entirely preventable outcome.
Adjust — Don't Stop
Continue basal insulin as long as the patient is conscious. As intake falls, reduce the dose — but never withdraw without specialist advice.
Reduce the Basal
As appetite declines, hepatic glucose output falls. An unchanged basal dose causes progressive overnight hypoglycaemia.
Stop the Bolus
Bolus insulin covers post-meal glucose. Without food, giving bolus insulin causes hypoglycaemia without any physiological purpose.
