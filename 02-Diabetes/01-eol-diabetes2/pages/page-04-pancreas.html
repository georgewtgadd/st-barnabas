Anatomy

The Pancreas

Normal blood glucose control depends on the pancreas acting as both an endocrine and an exocrine organ. Understanding this dual role explains why diabetes can arise from immune disease, insulin resistance or direct pancreatic damage.

🫀 Endocrine — Islets of Langerhans 🍽 Exocrine — Digestive Enzymes
🩸
Endocrine Gland
Insulin & Glucagon

Secretes hormones directly into the bloodstream via the Islets of Langerhans. Beta cells release insulin when glucose rises after eating, helping glucose move into muscle and fat cells and promoting glycogen storage in the liver. Alpha cells release glucagon when glucose falls, stimulating the liver to release stored glucose. This endocrine balance is the core of normal glucose homeostasis.

EoL Relevance Insulin-dependent patients cannot survive without some exogenous insulin — complete withdrawal causes DKA within hours to days.
🍽
Exocrine Gland
Digestive Enzymes

Produces digestive enzymes such as amylase, lipase and proteases that flow through ducts directly into the duodenum. These enzymes digest carbohydrate, fat and protein so nutrients can be absorbed. When exocrine tissue is damaged, digestion and nutrition suffer, and neighbouring islet cells may also be lost — one reason pancreatic disease can cause Type 3c diabetes.

EoL Relevance Pancreatic cancer affecting the exocrine tissue can simultaneously destroy Islets — causing Type 3c diabetes.
