Counter-Regulatory Hormones

Normal glucose homeostasis depends chiefly on insulin lowering glucose and glucagon raising it, but stress hormones such as adrenaline and cortisol can also drive blood sugar upward. Use the Glucose Elevator to see these effects in action.

↑
Adrenaline
Adrenal Medulla

Rapidly increases blood glucose to provide immediate energy for fight-or-flight. In EoL care, uncontrolled pain or agitation can trigger adrenaline surges — elevating glucose and exacerbating symptoms in diabetic patients.

↑
Cortisol & Steroids
Adrenal Cortex (steroids)

Corticosteroids increase hepatic glucose production and worsen insulin resistance. Steroid-induced diabetes refers to a new rise in glucose in someone without a previous diagnosis. Steroid-induced hyperglycaemia refers to the worsening of control in someone with pre-existing diabetes. Both require active monitoring and treatment adjustment.

↓
Insulin
Beta Cells (pancreas)

The body's primary glucose-lowering hormone — the "key" that unlocks cells to allow glucose entry for energy production. Without insulin, glucose accumulates in the blood while cells starve.

📦
Glucagon
Alpha Cells → Liver

Glucagon is released from alpha cells when blood glucose falls. It acts mainly on the liver to promote glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis, raising glucose between meals. In malnourished patients, however, glycogen stores may be depleted, so this rescue mechanism becomes unreliable.
