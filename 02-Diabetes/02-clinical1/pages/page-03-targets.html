Blood Glucose Zones

For patients in the terminal phase, glucose targets are deliberately relaxed. The goal is avoiding distressing symptoms at both extremes — not achieving a perfect reading.

20+ mmol/L
⚠️ DKA / HHS Risk
Severe hyperglycaemia — symptoms likely. Risk of DKA (Type 1) or HHS (Type 2). Consider whether hospital-level care aligns with the care plan.
15–20 mmol/L
🟡 Caution Zone
Elevated but not immediately dangerous. Monitor symptoms — thirst, confusion, dry mouth. Review medication regimen. Test once daily or when symptomatic.
6–15 mmol/L
✓ Comfort Target — Terminal Phase
This is the recommended target range. At 6–15 mmol/L, most patients will be free from the symptoms of both extremes. Finger-prick testing should be minimised — once daily or when symptomatic is usually sufficient.
Below 4 mmol/L
🔴 Hypoglycaemia
Treat immediately. Below 4 mmol/L causes confusion, sweating, seizures and loss of consciousness. This is preventable, reversible, and distressing — it must be avoided with appropriate medication rationalisation.
🩸
Testing Frequency
Minimise finger-prick testing. In the final days, once daily — or only when symptomatic — is usually sufficient. Testing causes distress without clinical benefit in the terminal phase.
📋
Symptom-Led Monitoring
If the patient appears suddenly confused, shaky, or disorientated, test first if possible — then treat. In the absence of equipment, treat presumptively based on clinical signs.
